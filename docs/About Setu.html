SmartAIthon 2026 · Disaster Prediction & Alert Systems

Setu — a disaster alert that's already on your phone before the disaster is.

Most alert systems need a live connection at the exact moment everything goes wrong. Setu flips that: real trained models watch the data, decide in advance, and quietly arm your phone while the network still works — so the alert can fire locally even if the network doesn't.

Monitoringlive data, online
Armedalert pre-staged on device
Network lostno server round-trip needed
Fired locallydevice acts on its own
This four-state sequence is still the entire product's demo spine. What changed in v2 is what decides when to arm: a real trained model per disaster type, not a hand-written threshold.
Framing

The one-line pitch

This is what goes on slide one.

Pitch line "Setu trains real machine learning models on years of historical AQI, cyclone, and flood data, uses them to pre-stage disaster alerts on your phone while the network still works, fires them locally the instant trigger conditions are met — no connection required — and relays new, unexpected hazard reports peer-to-peer when the network is down."

Three trained models, one spine: sense → predict → verify → act. Each disaster type gets its own model trained on its own historical data, because AQI, cyclones, and floods genuinely behave differently and deserve different evidence — but all three feed the same staging-and-firing pipeline downstream.

Honest precedent check

Naming what already exists, unprompted, makes you look informed rather than derivative.

What already exists Cell Broadcast systems (US Wireless Emergency Alerts, India's upcoming Cell Broadcast Alert System) already push pre-written templates by geography with no app or internet needed at delivery time. Trained ML models for AQI, cyclone tracks, and flood forecasting all exist in research and some commercial form — IMD, NASA, and various university groups have published work in each area individually. Bluetooth/offline mesh messaging for crisis use has prior art too (Bridgefy-style apps, student projects like UNIHACK 2026's "MeshMap").
What's actually new here Nobody combines three independently trained disaster models with a personalized, app-based pre-staging mechanism, a visible deterministic trust layer, and a local peer relay fallback in one consumer system. The pre-staging-before-disconnection pattern — deciding in advance, arming silently, firing without needing a server round-trip — is the genuinely uncommon piece. Frame it as: "a software-only, smarter, personalized version of what Cell Broadcast does at the network level, built on real trained models instead of manual thresholds, with a trust layer and offline fallback it doesn't have."
The technical core

Why three real, separately trained models

This is the single biggest change from v1, and the part of this project most worth doing properly — for the pitch, and because it's genuinely your first real ML build.

Why not one unified model AQI, cyclones, and floods are driven by different physical processes on different timescales. AQI is a slow chemical accumulation problem driven by traffic, weather stagnation, and seasonal burning. Cyclones are a pressure-and-wind-pattern problem tracked over days at sea. Floods are a rainfall-and-river-discharge accumulation problem. Forcing one model to learn all three at once would mean diluting it with mismatched signals — three separate models, each trained on its own relevant historical data, is both more accurate and more honest.
Why this is a good first ML project All three of these are tabular time-series prediction problems — given a table of recent numeric readings (AQI levels, rainfall, wind speed, pressure, and so on), predict a number or risk level some hours or days ahead. This is one of the most well-trodden, well-documented categories of machine learning there is. It doesn't require deep learning, large compute, or image/text data — gradient-boosted decision trees (XGBoost or LightGBM) handle this kind of data extremely well and are realistic to train, evaluate, and actually understand as a first model, with guidance the whole way.

Step by step, in plain terms

  • Collect: download the historical dataset for that disaster type (sources listed under each model below) — this is just files of past readings, nothing trained yet.This step is mostly downloading and inspecting — getting a feel for what the raw data actually looks like.
  • Clean: fix missing values, mismatched units, duplicate rows, and obviously broken readings.Real-world data is always messier than it looks at first — this step is unglamorous but determines whether anything downstream works.
  • Engineer features: turn raw readings into the inputs the model will actually learn from — e.g. not just "today's AQI" but "AQI 24 hours ago, rate of change over the last 3 days, wind speed, humidity, day of year."This is the step that most determines model quality — good features matter more than a fancier model.
  • Split by time: train on older data, test on newer data the model has never seen — never split randomly for time-series data, or the model can accidentally "cheat" by learning from the future.This is the single most important rule for doing this honestly — getting it wrong is the most common beginner mistake in time-series ML.
  • Train: the model (XGBoost/LightGBM) looks at the training data and learns the relationship between the input features and the real historical outcome.This is usually the fastest step in wall-clock time — minutes, not hours, for datasets this size.
  • Evaluate honestly: run the trained model against the held-out test data it never saw, and measure how often it was actually right.This produces the real precision/recall/false-positive numbers — see the evaluation section below.
  • Wire in: the trained model becomes a function the live Stage 1 pipeline calls with current conditions, returning a prediction and a confidence level.From this point on, the rest of the system treats it the same way v1 treated the old rule-based threshold — just with a real model underneath now.
Model 1 of 3 — build this first

AQI prediction model

The most approachable of the three. Strong, dense, free historical data and a well-understood prediction task.

1

What it predicts

Regression

Given recent AQI and weather readings for a city, predict the AQI level 24–72 hours ahead, plus a derived risk tier (good / moderate / poor / severe).

  • Inputs: recent AQI history, wind speed/direction, humidity, temperature, time of year (captures seasonal burning patterns)All freely available, hourly, for major Indian cities.
  • Output: predicted AQI value + tier, with a confidence range
Data sources CPCB historical archives OpenAQ AQICN / waqi.info Open-Meteo (weather history)
Model 2 of 3

Cyclone risk model

Fewer rows than AQI data, but very clean, well-studied, and exactly the kind of structured tabular problem gradient-boosted trees handle well.

2

What it predicts

Classification + regression

Given a forming or tracked storm system's current readings, predict landfall likelihood for coastal regions and intensity trend over the next 24–48 hours.

  • Inputs: central pressure, wind speed, pressure drop, track position and direction, sea surface temperature, seasonThese are the same predictors used in published IMD-data research on this exact problem.
  • Output: landfall probability for nearby coastal zones + predicted intensity category
Data sources IMD RSMC New Delhi best-track data (1990–2017+) IBTrACS (NOAA)

Decision

Location coverage strategy

You wanted free-text "enter any location" input. Here's how that's honestly possible without faking accuracy somewhere nobody can see.

The constraint A trained model only knows what it saw in training. A model trained on Delhi/Mumbai-style metro data will not give honest, accurate predictions if silently pointed at a small town's live data it never learned from. Rather than fake universal coverage, the location search stays fully open — but what happens underneath is clearly tiered and disclosed.
Delhi Mumbai Bangalore Chennai Kolkata Hyderabad + any other location → labeled fallback

The thing v1 was missing

Backtest & evaluation — proving it actually works

Right now, nothing proves any of this is good at predicting. This section fixes that, and produces your strongest demo moment.

E1

Standard evaluation metrics

Critical, previously missing

For each model, measured honestly against held-out historical data the model never trained on.

  • Accuracy / error margin on the predicted value itself (how close was the predicted AQI to the real one)
  • Precision — when the model predicted a spike/disaster, how often was it actually right
  • Recall — of the real historical spikes/disasters, how many did the model successfully flag in advance
  • False positive rate — how often it cried wolfThis number matters enormously for a real alert system — too many false alarms destroys user trust, and judges who think about real deployment will ask about this.
E2

Historical replay demo

New in v2 — flagship demo moment

Instead of only a synthetic "simulate a disaster" button, let a judge replay a real, named historical event and watch the system correctly predict it before the actual event happened in history.

  • Pick one well-documented real past event per model — e.g. a real Delhi/NCR severe smog episode for AQI, a real named cyclone (such as Amphan) for the cyclone model, a real flood event from the curated flood datasetThis is strictly more convincing than a synthetic demo, because it's checkable against real history, not a script you wrote yourself.
  • Replay feeds the model historical data exactly as it would have arrived in real time, and shows prediction confidence rising as the real event approachedMirrors the same Monitoring → Armed → Fired sequence, but against real history instead of a simulation.
Why this matters beyond the pitch This is also just good practice for learning ML properly — a model you can't honestly evaluate isn't one you can trust, regardless of how it's used. Building this evaluation habit now will serve you on every future ML project, not just this one.
Reference

ML terms used above, plainly explained

Since this is your first model — quick definitions for anything above that might be unfamiliar, in plain language.

XGBoost / LightGBM
Two popular, well-tested libraries that build "gradient-boosted decision trees" — essentially, lots of small decision rules combined together, each one correcting the mistakes of the ones before it. They're a strong default choice for tabular data like weather readings, fast to train, and produce results that can be inspected and explained.
Features
The individual pieces of input information given to the model — e.g. "yesterday's AQI" or "wind speed" are each one feature. Choosing good features matters more to the result than almost anything else in the process.
Train / test split
The historical data gets divided into two parts: one the model learns from (train), and one it never sees until evaluation time (test), so you can honestly check whether it actually learned something general, rather than just memorizing the data.
Precision vs recall
Precision asks "when the model raised an alarm, how often was it right?" Recall asks "of all the real disasters that happened, how many did the model catch?" A model can be excellent at one and poor at the other — both numbers matter, especially together.
Overfitting
When a model essentially memorizes the exact training data instead of learning a general pattern — it looks great on data it's already seen and performs poorly on anything new. The train/test split is the main defense against this.
Backtest
Running the trained model against real past data to see what it would have predicted at the time, then checking that against what actually happened — this is where the historical replay demo comes from.
System — Stage 0

Data ingestion

The foundation both the live dashboard and all three trained models depend on.

0

Live regional data feed

Foundation

Pull real, live weather and air-quality data on a recurring schedule for covered cities, and keep a rolling history — this feeds both the live dashboard and the running input to each trained model.

  • Scheduled polling job (every few minutes) pulling current conditions, not just a single fetch on page load
  • Store readings in a simple time-series table keyed by region and timestamp
  • Normalize units and categories on the way in, matching the same feature format each trained model expects

System — Stage 1

Predictive pre-staging engine

The headline mechanism. Now driven by the three trained models above instead of hand-written thresholds.

1

Model-driven risk detection

Headline featureChanged

Live data flows into the relevant trained model (AQI, cyclone, or flood) for a given covered region, producing a prediction and confidence score on a rolling basis.

  • Each trained model runs against current live data continuously, not just on-demandThis replaces v1's manual rate-of-change rules entirely.
  • A confidence/risk score is attached to every predictionNow genuinely model-derived, not a hand-set threshold — this is what makes the "AI predicts" claim accurate rather than aspirational.
  • Threshold crossing triggers the next step automatically
1b

Alert package generation

Core

Once a model crosses threshold, generate the alert content — severity tier, plain-language explanation, recommended action — via a fast LLM call.

  • Tiered severity attached to every generated package
  • Plain-language text generated per-incident, referencing the actual model output (e.g. "predicted AQI of 340 in the next 18 hours, driven by stagnant wind conditions")Grounding the LLM's text in the real model number, not letting it invent the justification.
  • Recommended action included in the same package
  • Use a fast model (e.g. a Haiku-class model) with streaming output for any live demo callsKeeps demo pacing tight — text should visibly appear immediately, not sit in silence for several seconds.
1c

Silent pre-staging on device

The actual trick

Push the generated alert package to the user's device while connectivity is still available, and store it locally — staged and waiting.

  • Package stored in browser local storage / IndexedDB on the device
  • Local trigger condition attached: a time window, a local condition check, or "if no further server update arrives by time X, surface anyway"
  • Visible "armed" state shown in the app UI the moment staging completes
1d

Local fire, independent of network

The payoff

When the trigger condition is met, the device surfaces the already-staged alert using logic that runs entirely on-device.

  • Service worker checks staged packages against trigger conditions on a timer
  • Local notification fires using already-known content — no fetch call required to decide
System — Stage 2

Trust & verification engine

Re-split from v1: a deterministic algorithm now decides the actual confidence number, and the LLM's job is narrowed to explaining it.

2

Citizen hazard reporting

Core

A simple, fast form anyone can use to report something happening right now near them.

  • Hazard type, location, short description, optional photo
2b

Deterministic confidence scoring

Changed — algorithm, not LLM

A plain rules-and-math algorithm computes the confidence score — no LLM involved in deciding the number itself.

  • Cross-reference report location/time against Stage 0's live data feed (e.g. does live rainfall data support a flooding report)
  • Cross-reference against other nearby reports in a recent time window — count and proximity computed directly
  • Factor in reporter history if availableAll of this is ordinary computation — distance, time windows, counts — nothing here needs an LLM, and that's the point: a deterministic number can't hallucinate or vary between runs.
2c

LLM explains, doesn't decide

Core

Once the algorithm has produced a confidence number, an LLM call narrates why — turning the computed factors into a readable reasoning trace.

  • "3 reports within 10 minutes + live rainfall data confirms active precipitation = 92% confidence, escalating" — written out, not hiddenThis is still the best demo moment in the whole product: judges watch reasoning narrated over a real number, not a number a language model invented on the spot.
  • Verified high-confidence reports feed into the same pre-staging pipeline as Stage 1Same delivery mechanism, different trigger source.
System — Stage 3

Offline relay (mesh fallback)

Kept, by your call, as a real feature worth building. Sequenced last since it's the most technically uncertain piece.

3

Peer-to-peer relay, small scale

Core, scoped narrow

A real, working, narrow proof-of-concept of phone-to-phone (or tab-to-tab) message relay — not a claim of citywide mesh coverage.

  • WebRTC or Web Bluetooth connection between two to three pre-paired devicesBest demoed on Android Chrome, where Web Bluetooth actually works.
  • A verified alert relays from a connected device to a disconnected one nearby
  • Explicitly framed in the pitch as a scalable proof-of-concept, not production-scale mesh
Risk reframed for your actual format Crowded-venue radio interference is a known risk for in-person hackathon demos, but since you're competing in virtual hackathons, you control your own demo environment fully — this risk doesn't really apply to you. The remaining real limitation is platform support: Web Bluetooth works in Chrome on Android, not in Safari on iOS, and isn't designed for always-on background relay at scale. State that plainly; it doesn't need a fallback recording given your format.
